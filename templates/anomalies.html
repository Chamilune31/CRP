Anomaly Detection - three models, three opinions

An anomaly is a row whose forecast or actual sales looks unusually different from the rest. Three different methods are run side-by-side so we can cross-check their opinions before validating a forecast.

How to read this page - order of inspection

1
Read Section 1 first - the simplest of the three (statistics). It gives you a baseline: "anything outside this band is unusual".
2
Then Section 2 (Isolation Forest). A smarter, AI-based model that catches multi-dimensional weirdness the statistical test cannot. Compare its anomalies to those of Section 1.
3
Finally Section 3 (Logistic Regression). A supervised model trained on past data - gives a probability for every row. Use this to prioritise: highest probabilities first.
4
Look for agreement. When two or three models flag the same row, the evidence is strong. When only one flags it, treat it as a candidate for manual check.

1Confidence-Interval method (the statistical baseline)

In plain words We compute the average ("mean") and the spread ("standard deviation") of all the changes in Gross Sales between snapshots. We then draw an invisible band around the average. Any row whose change falls outside the band is called an anomaly. We use a level of {{ (ci_metrics.level * 100) | round(0) }}% - the value the reference study identified as the best trade-off between catching anomalies and avoiding false alarms.

2Isolation Forest (the AI catcher)

In plain words Imagine playing "20 questions" to identify each row. Strange rows can be isolated with very few questions - that's what makes them "anomalies". The model looks at four things at once (Gross Sales change, Gross Sales total, Sales-Out change, Sales-Out total) so it catches weirdness no single number can. We tell it to flag the most unusual {{ (if_metrics.contamination * 100) | round(0) }}% of the inconsistent rows.

3Logistic Regression (the trained classifier)

In plain words Unlike the previous two models, this one is trained on past data: we showed it many examples of "inconsistent" and "consistent" rows and it learned the pattern. For every row it now outputs a probability between 0% and 100% - your prioritisation score. It uses an 80/20 time-aware split, meaning the test set is strictly more recent than the training set (no peeking at the future).
How to read the chart Once again, grey = normal, red = flagged. Because this model uses a probability threshold of 50%, it tends to flag more rows than Isolation Forest. 4How to combine the three opinions

Decision rule
  • All three flag the row → strong signal. Definitely review.
  • Two flag it → likely anomaly. Worth a quick look.
  • Only one flags it → could be a false alarm. Use the per-item page to investigate before acting.
  • None flag it → safe to validate.

The Suspicious Items ranking already does this blending for you and gives one composite score per item.
